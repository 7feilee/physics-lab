Mercury's orbit is an ellipse, and that ellipse slowly turns. Its closest point to the Sun — the **perihelion** — creeps around the sky, tracing a rosette over the centuries. Newton's gravity predicts most of that creep, from the tugs of the other planets: about 532 arcseconds per century. The telescopes said 575. The difference is **43 arcseconds per century** — forty-three sixty-thousandths of a degree, every hundred years, left over after every known planet had been accounted for. It is one of the smallest discrepancies in the history of science, and it brought down Newton. This is the story of how a sliver of an angle, stubbornly refusing to vanish, forced us to abandon flat space and gravity-as-a-force, and replace them with the curved spacetime of general relativity.

## A wobble that wouldn't go away

By the mid-1800s, celestial mechanics was the crown jewel of physics — so precise it was almost a branch of pure mathematics. Astronomers could predict eclipses centuries ahead and chart the planets to a whisker. So when Urbain Le Verrier, the finest celestial mechanic alive, carefully summed up every gravitational pull on Mercury and *still* came up 38 arcseconds short of the observed precession (later refined to 43″), it was not noise. It was a precise, reproducible anomaly in the most trusted theory in all of science.

This is the discovery chain in its purest form: a measurement of exquisite precision, disagreeing with prediction by a tiny but rock-solid amount that survives every recheck. The question was not whether the 43″ was real — it plainly was. The question was *what it meant*.

## The hunt for Vulcan: a missing object?

Le Verrier already knew, from triumphant experience, what an anomaly like this usually means: a **missing object**. Just years earlier, the planet Uranus had been straying from its Newtonian path. Le Verrier had assumed an unseen planet was pulling on it, calculated exactly where that planet must sit, and within hours of looking, astronomers found **Neptune** less than a degree from his prediction. It was the most spectacular confirmation Newtonian gravity ever received — physics as prophecy.

So Le Verrier made the obvious bet again. Something unseen must be tugging Mercury's perihelion around. A small planet, or a ring of asteroids, orbiting *inside* Mercury, close to the Sun where the glare would hide it. He even named it: **Vulcan**. The hunt was on. For half a century, observers scoured the Sun's edge during eclipses and reported flickering dots. Every claimed sighting dissolved under scrutiny. Vulcan was never there.

:::history Same symptom, opposite disease
Uranus and Mercury presented the *identical* symptom — an orbit straying from Newton's prediction. For Uranus the cure was a missing object (Neptune), a triumph *for* Newton. For Mercury the same cure failed completely: there was no Vulcan, anywhere, ever. The discipline of discovery is telling these two cases apart, and you usually cannot know in advance which you face. You must hunt the missing object honestly — and only when it is decisively absent do you earn the right to suspect the law itself.
:::

By around 1900, the verdict was in. There is no planet inside Mercury's orbit. The "missing object" explanation was dead. The boring answers were exhausted: not the other planets, not an unseen world, not the Sun's slight bulge (too small), not measurement error (the 43″ was iron-solid). Only one possibility was left, the one no one wanted — that Newton's law of gravity, the most successful theory humanity had, was *itself* subtly wrong near the Sun.

## Einstein nails it — exactly

In November 1915, Einstein completed general relativity. Gravity, he proposed, is not a force reaching across empty space. It is the **curvature of spacetime**: mass tells spacetime how to bend, and the bends tell matter how to move. A planet "orbiting" is simply following the straightest possible path through a geometry warped by the Sun.

Crucially — by the correspondence principle — in weak gravity and at low speeds this curved-spacetime picture reproduces Newton almost perfectly. It keeps every one of Newton's centuries of successes. But "almost" is the whole point. Near the Sun, where gravity is strongest and Mercury (the innermost, fastest planet) feels it most, general relativity predicts a *small extra* precession that Newton's flat-space theory misses.

Einstein computed it. The answer came out to **43 arcseconds per century** — not roughly, but on the nose, with no free parameters, no fudge factor, nothing to adjust. He had not gone looking to explain Mercury; the number simply fell out of his geometry. He later wrote that the discovery left him with heart palpitations; for days he was "beside himself with joy." A theory built from a thought about falling elevators had, on its own, dissolved a sixty-year-old anomaly to the last digit.

:::deep Why the extra 43″?
In Newton's universe, an isolated planet's ellipse stays perfectly fixed — its perihelion never moves at all. The other planets nudge it around, but the underlying orbit is a closed, non-precessing ellipse. General relativity changes this at the root: even a *lone* planet around a lone star precesses, because spacetime curvature subtly alters the inverse-square pull, so the orbit doesn't quite close on itself and the ellipse slowly rotates. The effect grows with stronger gravity and higher speed — which is why tiny, fast, Sun-hugging Mercury shows it most, and distant slow planets barely at all.
:::

## A revolution, then more confirmations

Mercury was retrodiction — explaining a known anomaly. The real test of a new theory is *new* predictions, and general relativity delivered. It said the Sun should bend starlight passing its edge by 1.75 arcseconds, twice the Newtonian value; the 1919 eclipse expedition measured it and made Einstein world-famous overnight. It predicted that clocks run slower in stronger gravity, confirmed in the lab and now corrected for in every GPS satellite. It predicted black holes, gravitational lensing, and ripples in spacetime — **gravitational waves**, detected at last in 2015, a century after the theory and from the collision of two black holes the theory had also predicted. Every one of these grew from the same root: 43 stubborn arcseconds that would not go away.

:::key Rule out the mundane, then break the law
Mercury's wobble teaches the central discipline of discovery. The same anomaly can mean "a missing object" (Neptune — the law is fine) or "a broken law" (Mercury — the theory must change), and the two look identical at first. You earn the right to overturn a great theory *only* by first exhausting the conventional answer — chasing Vulcan to its grave — and then demanding that whatever replaces the old law reproduce its every success while resolving the anomaly exactly. Einstein's 43″, with no adjustable knob, did precisely that. A precise number, honestly chased, can be worth more than any beautiful idea.
:::
