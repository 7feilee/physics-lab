Deep in a South Dakota gold mine, in a tank holding 600 tonnes of dry-cleaning fluid, Ray Davis spent thirty years counting neutrinos from the Sun. The Sun is a fusion reactor, and the nuclear reactions that power it are understood in beautiful detail — well enough to predict, to maybe 20%, how many neutrinos should stream out of its core and pass through Davis's tank each day. Davis caught them, one atom at a time. And year after year, he caught only about **one-third** of the number the theory demanded. The other two-thirds were simply missing. For decades this was treated as an embarrassment — surely Davis had made a mistake, or the solar models were off. He had not, and they were not. The missing neutrinos were the first solid crack in the Standard Model of particle physics, and they revealed that the ghostliest particles in nature are not, after all, massless.

## Counting the Sun's neutrinos

The Sun shines by fusing hydrogen into helium, and that fusion unavoidably emits **neutrinos** — wisps of a particle so reluctant to interact that they pour straight through the Sun, through space, through the Earth, and through you, trillions per second, almost never touching anything. That ghostliness makes them precious: a neutrino from the solar core reaches us in eight minutes, carrying a direct report from the heart of the Sun, where light itself takes tens of thousands of years to crawl out.

It also makes them maddening to detect. Davis's **Homestake experiment**, begun in the 1960s, relied on the rare occasion when a neutrino converts a chlorine atom in his cleaning fluid into a radioactive argon atom. He would let the tank sit, then flush it and count the handful of argon atoms produced — *literally* counting individual atoms from a tank the size of a swimming pool. The expected rate, from the detailed solar-fusion models of John Bahcall, was solid. Davis's measured rate came in at roughly a third of it. The **solar neutrino problem** was born.

## A discrepancy that wouldn't die

For thirty years this deficit refused to go away, and for thirty years most physicists assumed someone had blundered. The chain of suspicion ran exactly as the discipline of discovery prescribes: *exhaust the boring explanations first.*

Was Davis's exquisitely difficult experiment wrong, miscounting atoms? It was checked and rechecked; it held. Was the solar model wrong — the Sun's core cooler than thought, making fewer neutrinos? This was the favourite escape for years, but the neutrino output depends steeply on the core temperature, and independent measurements (including, later, the Sun's vibrations, *helioseismology*) pinned the temperature down and confirmed the models. As other detectors using *different* target materials came online — gallium experiments, and Japan's water-based Kamiokande — they all saw a deficit too. The shortfall was real, reproducible, and seen by independent techniques. One by one, the mundane explanations died.

:::warn Don't blame the hard experiment first
For decades the easy assumption was that Davis — doing something almost absurdly difficult, counting argon atoms one by one — must be the weak link, or that the solar theorists had slipped. Both were treated as more likely wrong than the physics being incomplete. Both were, in the end, right. The careful experiment and the careful theory each survived every audit; it was a cherished *assumption* about neutrinos that had to give. When a stubborn anomaly outlives every attempt to pin it on experimental error, that is precisely when you should start taking it as a signal.
:::

## The bold idea: neutrinos change flavour

There was one escape that pointed not to error but to new physics. Neutrinos come in three types, or **flavours**: electron, muon, and tau. The Sun's fusion makes *electron* neutrinos, and Davis's chlorine could only catch electron neutrinos. So suppose the neutrinos don't vanish at all — suppose they **change flavour** in flight, morphing from electron-type into muon- and tau-type on the eight-minute journey from the Sun. Davis would then catch only the third that still happened to be electron-flavoured when they arrived, while the other two-thirds sailed past his detector invisibly. Not missing — *disguised*.

This idea, **neutrino oscillation**, had been floated as early as the late 1950s and 60s. It is genuinely strange: a particle that smoothly oscillates from one identity into another and back as it travels, like a struck tuning fork beating between notes. And it carried a sharp, non-negotiable consequence buried in the quantum mechanics. For neutrinos to oscillate between flavours, the different flavours must have *different masses* — which means the neutrinos cannot all be massless. In the Standard Model as written, neutrinos were assumed to have *exactly zero* mass. Oscillation could only happen if that assumption was wrong.

## SNO and Super-Kamiokande clinch it

A beautiful idea is not enough; you need an experiment that can catch flavour change in the act. Two did, around 2000.

**Super-Kamiokande** in Japan, watching neutrinos produced in the atmosphere, found that muon neutrinos arriving from *below* — having travelled through the whole Earth — were depleted compared with those from overhead, depending on the distance they'd flown. That distance-dependence is the fingerprint of oscillation.

The decisive blow came from the **Sudbury Neutrino Observatory (SNO)** in Canada, using 1,000 tonnes of *heavy* water. SNO's masterstroke was that it could measure two things at once: the flux of electron neutrinos *alone*, and — through a different reaction — the flux of *all three flavours combined*. The result was the smoking gun. The electron-neutrino count was low, matching Davis's deficit. But the all-flavours count came out at the *full* value Bahcall's solar model had predicted, decades earlier. The neutrinos were never missing. They had simply changed flavour on the way, and SNO caught the complete set. The Sun's books balanced exactly.

:::key Believe a careful experiment over a beautiful theory
The Standard Model said neutrinos are massless. It was elegant, successful, and — on this point — wrong. A precise count of solar neutrinos, sustained against decades of doubt, said otherwise, and the experiment won. The discovery lesson is to trust a careful, reproducible measurement even when it contradicts your most polished theory. Davis's stubborn factor of three, dismissed for a generation as a likely mistake, turned out to be the first direct evidence that neutrinos have mass — the first confirmed crack in the Standard Model, and a sign that an even deeper theory of particles is still waiting to be found. Davis and Koshiba shared the 2002 Nobel Prize; the oscillation discovery brought another in 2015.
:::
