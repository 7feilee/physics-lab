Every revolution in this track began as a number that didn't quite fit — Mercury's 43 arcseconds, the missing two-thirds of the Sun's neutrinos, the rotation curves that wouldn't droop. At the time, each looked like a minor embarrassment, a discrepancy most physicists expected would dissolve into a calibration error or a forgotten correction. Some did. The ones that didn't reshaped reality. So the natural question for anyone who wants to *discover* new physics, rather than merely read about it, is: where are today's stubborn numbers? Which of the anomalies on the books right now might be the next 43 arcseconds — and which are tomorrow's loose fibre-optic cable? This lesson is a tour of the live frontier, with the honesty such a tour demands: **none of these is confirmed.** Every one might evaporate. That is exactly what makes them worth watching.

## A caution before we start

The discipline from the playbook applies in full, and never more than here. A live anomaly is a discrepancy that has *not yet* run the full gauntlet — it has not reached $5\sigma$, or has not been independently replicated, or its theoretical baseline is still disputed, or all three. Most anomalies at this stage are destined to die: a $3\sigma$ effect (about 1-in-740 odds of being a fluke) evaporates more often than it survives, and the look-elsewhere effect manufactures "hints" wherever people look hard enough. Treat everything below as *unconfirmed*. The skill is not to believe them, but to weigh them — to judge which look promising and why, and what it would take to clinch each one.

## The muon's wobble: $g\!-\!2$

The muon is the electron's heavier cousin, and like the electron it is a tiny magnet whose strength is captured by a number called $g$. Dirac's theory says $g=2$ exactly; quantum field theory adds small corrections from the cloud of virtual particles a muon stirs up, pushing $g$ a hair above 2. That excess — the "anomalous magnetic moment," $g\!-\!2$ — can be both *calculated* to extraordinary precision and *measured* to extraordinary precision, making it one of the sharpest confrontations between theory and experiment in all of physics. If unknown particles lurk in that virtual cloud, they would nudge the measured value away from the prediction.

For years, the measured $g\!-\!2$ sat tantalisingly above the standard prediction by around $4\sigma$ — enticing, but short of discovery, and the kind of gap that has vanished before. Fermilab's experiment has since pinned the *measurement* down superbly. The catch is on the theory side: a competing way of computing the standard-model baseline (using lattice QCD) shifts the predicted value, and depending on which calculation you trust, the anomaly is either striking or largely gone. So $g\!-\!2$ is a vivid lesson in a subtlety the older cases share — an anomaly is a gap between measurement *and prediction*, and a prediction can be wrong too. **What would clinch it:** the theory community converging on a single, agreed standard-model value. Until the prediction is settled, the gap can't be read.

## The Hubble tension

This one is harder to wave away, because it pits two excellent measurements of the *same number* against each other. The number is the **Hubble constant** $H_0$, the present expansion rate of the universe. There are two clean ways to get it.

- **The early-universe route:** read $H_0$ from the cosmic microwave background — the relic light of the infant cosmos — by fitting the standard cosmological model. This gives about **67** km/s per megaparsec.
- **The late-universe route:** measure it directly and locally, by climbing the cosmic distance ladder (Cepheid stars calibrating Type Ia supernovae). This gives about **73**.

Those two numbers should agree. They differ by roughly 8%, at a significance that has climbed past $5\sigma$ as both methods have sharpened. This is the **Hubble tension**, and it is among the most credible anomalies in physics today precisely because it is not one shaky measurement but two independently precise ones that *refuse to reconcile*. Either there is an unknown systematic hiding in one of these heroic measurement chains (the boring explanation, still not ruled out) — or the standard cosmological model is missing something between the early and late universe: new physics in the dark sector, in the early expansion, or somewhere stranger. **What would clinch it:** fully independent rungs on the ladder — gravitational-wave "standard sirens," lensing time-delays, the tip of the red-giant branch — converging on one value and isolating where the discrepancy truly lives.

## The W-boson mass — and how anomalies fade

The **W boson** is a carrier of the weak force, and its mass is predicted by the Standard Model once a few other quantities are fixed. In 2022 the CDF collaboration announced an extraordinarily precise measurement of that mass — and it came out *high*, in tension with the prediction at around $7\sigma$, which on its face would be a blazing discovery. But here the discipline bites hard: the CDF value disagrees not only with theory but with *other experiments'* measurements of the same mass, including a later, more recent result that sits much closer to the Standard Model. When precise measurements of the same quantity disagree with *each other*, an undiagnosed systematic in one of them is the leading suspect, not new physics. The W-mass anomaly is, as of now, unresolved and widely regarded with caution — a reminder that a huge sigma counts for little until independent experiments agree.

:::warn Many hints fade — and that is the system working
The B-meson decays are the cautionary tale. For several years, certain rare decays of B mesons showed a pattern of deviations from the Standard Model — including hints that the particles weren't treating electrons and muons even-handedly, which would have been spectacular. Excitement built. Then, with more data and improved analysis, the most striking of these (the "lepton-universality" hints) **faded back toward the Standard Model**. The anomaly that didn't survive is not a failure of physics — it is the method working *exactly as designed*. Demanding $5\sigma$, independent replication, and the death of every mundane explanation is precisely what stops us from canonising flukes. Most hints are supposed to die. The few that don't are the ones worth everything.
:::

## How to bet

So how should a would-be discoverer weigh these? The lessons of this whole track give a checklist, and the live anomalies sort themselves by it:

- **Is the baseline solid?** An anomaly is only as trustworthy as the prediction it deviates from. The Hubble tension scores well — both sides are direct measurements. The muon $g\!-\!2$ scores worse *right now*, not because the measurement is weak but because the standard-model prediction is in dispute. A gap you can't trust the prediction half of is not yet a gap.
- **Is it independently replicated?** A discrepancy seen by one experiment, one method, is on probation; one seen by several techniques that could each fail differently is far stronger. The Hubble tension is corroborated by multiple ladders; the CDF W-mass result is contradicted by others — a decisive difference in credibility.
- **Could it be a systematic?** Always the leading suspect. The 2011 "faster-than-light neutrinos" were a loose cable. When measurements of the *same* quantity disagree (W mass), bet on a hidden systematic before betting on new physics.
- **Missing object, or broken law?** Even if real, ask which kind it is — a particle we forgot to include, or a law that needs replacing — and resist whichever answer you're rooting for.

By this reckoning the Hubble tension currently looks the most robust of the live anomalies — two precise, independent measurements that stubbornly disagree, the most $5\sigma$-credible of the bunch — while $g\!-\!2$ waits on a theory verdict, the W mass waits on experiments to agree, and the B-meson hints have largely receded. But betting is not knowing, and any of these judgments could be overturned next year. That uncertainty is not a flaw in the field; it *is* the frontier.

:::key The next revolution may be in today's error bars
Every confirmed revolution in this track was once exactly what these anomalies are now: an unconfirmed discrepancy that most experts expected to vanish. The art of discovery is to take such gaps seriously *without* fooling yourself — to chase them with precision, exhaust the boring explanations, demand replication and $5\sigma$, and let most of them die so that the rare survivor can be trusted completely. Somewhere in a plot of residuals being made right now, in a tension between two careful numbers, in a bump just shy of significance, tomorrow's physics may already be sitting — small, stubborn, and waiting for someone disciplined enough to believe it only once it has earned belief. Watch the decimal places. That is where revolutions hide.
:::
