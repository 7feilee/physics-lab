By the 1990s cosmologists thought they had the universe's broad story straight. It began hot and dense, it expanded, and gravity — the only long-range force that matters at cosmic scales — should be gently slowing that expansion down, every galaxy tugging on every other. The only real question was whether gravity would eventually halt the expansion and reverse it, or merely slow it forever. Two teams set out to measure the deceleration. What they found instead, in 1998, was the most stunning surprise in modern physics: the expansion is not slowing at all. It is **speeding up**. Something is pushing the universe apart, overwhelming all the gravity of all the matter in it — and we have almost no idea what it is.

## The 1998 surprise

The measurement hinged on the standardisable candles from the stellar-death lesson: **Type Ia supernovae**, exploding white dwarfs that all reach nearly the same peak brightness because they all detonate at the Chandrasekhar mass. Knowing their true brightness, you can turn how *faint* one looks into how *far* away it is. And from its redshift you know how much the universe has expanded since its light set out. Collect supernovae at many distances and you reconstruct the expansion history — how the scale factor $a(t)$ has actually changed over billions of years.

Two rival groups did exactly this, expecting to measure how much the expansion had slowed. Distant supernovae should look slightly *brighter* than a constant-speed expansion would predict, because the universe used to expand faster and had braked. Instead the distant supernovae came in consistently *dimmer* — farther away — than even a coasting universe should allow. The only explanation: over the last several billion years, the expansion has been **accelerating**. The galaxies are flying apart ever faster. The discovery earned the 2011 Nobel Prize, and it rewrote cosmology. Gravity had a rival, and it was winning.

## The cosmological constant and vacuum energy

What could push space apart? The leading candidate is an idea Einstein himself introduced — and then abandoned in embarrassment. When he wrote down general relativity, his equations insisted the universe must expand or contract, contradicting the then-fashionable belief in a static cosmos. To force a static solution, he added a term, the **cosmological constant** $\Lambda$, a kind of constant energy built into space itself. When Hubble showed the universe *was* expanding, Einstein dropped $\Lambda$, reportedly calling it his greatest blunder.

The 1998 discovery resurrected it. A cosmological constant behaves like an energy that fills every cubic metre of empty space at a fixed density — and, bizarrely, comes with a **negative pressure**, a kind of tension. In general relativity, pressure gravitates, and negative pressure pushes *outward*, gravitationally repulsive. Here is the crucial wrinkle: as the universe expands, ordinary matter dilutes (the same atoms spread over more volume), but the energy of empty space stays at the same density — more space simply means more vacuum energy. So early on, dense matter dominated and gravity slowed the expansion; but as expansion thinned the matter out, the unchanging vacuum energy eventually took over, and its outward push won. That is why the acceleration switched on only a few billion years ago. We call this mysterious repulsive energy **dark energy**, and the cosmological constant is its simplest description.

## The cosmic energy budget

Putting all the evidence together — supernovae, the cosmic microwave background, the cosmic web, galaxy surveys — cosmologists can now weigh the universe's entire contents. The inventory is humbling:

| Component | Share of the universe | What it is |
|---|---|---|
| Ordinary matter | ~5% | Atoms: stars, gas, planets, you |
| Dark matter | ~27% | Unknown, gravitating, invisible |
| Dark energy | ~68% | Unknown, repulsive, fills empty space |

Read that table slowly. Everything we have ever seen, touched, or built — every star and galaxy, every atom in every body — is about **5%** of the universe. Another 27% is the dark matter of the previous lesson, of unknown identity. And the dominant 68%, the majority of all the energy there is, is dark energy, which we understand even less. We live in a cosmos overwhelmingly made of two things we cannot identify. The era of thinking we knew what the universe is made of is over.

:::key The 5% universe
The ordinary matter of stars, planets, and people makes up roughly one-twentieth of the cosmic energy budget. Dark matter and dark energy — together 95% — remain unidentified. Modern cosmology is extraordinarily precise about *how much* of each there is, and almost entirely ignorant of *what* the dark sector actually is.
:::

## The fate of the universe

Dark energy doesn't just describe the present; it dictates the future. As long as it behaves like a true cosmological constant — a fixed energy density forever — the expansion will keep accelerating, and the long-term outlook is bleak and lonely. Distant galaxies will recede faster and faster, their light stretched into oblivion, until one by one they slip beyond our horizon and vanish from view. The sky will empty. Stars will burn out, no new ones will form, and the universe will drift toward a cold, dark, dilute equilibrium — the **heat death**, or "Big Freeze," in which nothing interesting can ever happen again.

A grimmer possibility lurks if dark energy is *not* constant but strengthens over time (a scenario called "phantom" dark energy). Then the acceleration runs away without limit, and the repulsion eventually overwhelms gravity on smaller and smaller scales: first galaxy clusters are torn apart, then galaxies, then solar systems, then planets, and in the final instant atoms themselves are ripped to shreds. This is the **Big Rip**, in which the expansion shatters everything. Which fate awaits depends on a property of dark energy we are only beginning to measure — and recent surveys hint, tantalisingly and not yet conclusively, that dark energy might be subtly changing with time after all.

## The deepest puzzle in physics

If the cosmological constant is vacuum energy, we ought to be able to *calculate* it. Quantum field theory says empty space is not empty — it seethes with the zero-point energy of every field, a roiling sea of virtual particles. Add up that vacuum energy and you get a prediction for $\Lambda$. The trouble is that the prediction is wrong by a factor of about $10^{120}$ — a one followed by a hundred and twenty zeros. It is, by a wide margin, the worst quantitative prediction in the history of science. Some unknown mechanism must cancel that gigantic theoretical vacuum energy almost perfectly, leaving the tiny but nonzero amount we observe. *Why* it cancels to 120 decimal places but not to 121 is the **cosmological constant problem**, and it is widely regarded as the deepest unsolved puzzle in all of physics.

:::deep An honest cliff-edge
We can measure dark energy's effect with exquisite precision and predict its theoretical value not at all — the two disagree by $10^{120}$. This is not a small loose end; it is a signpost that something fundamental about how quantum mechanics and gravity fit together is missing from our picture. The accelerating universe may be the most important clue we have to the physics beyond what we know.
:::

Cosmology has handed us a strange and magnificent inheritance. We have mapped the universe's history from a fraction of a second after the Big Bang to its accelerating present, weighed its contents to the percent, and traced the growth of every galaxy from ripples in the primordial glow. And in doing so we have discovered that 95% of the universe is made of substances we cannot name, that its expansion is speeding up for reasons we cannot calculate, and that its ultimate fate hinges on a number we are still struggling to measure. The map is breathtakingly detailed. The territory, it turns out, is still mostly dark — and that, for a curious mind, is the most exciting news of all.
