Run the expanding universe backwards far enough and you reach a state with no stars, no atoms, not even neutral matter — just a blindingly hot, dense plasma of fundamental particles filling all of space. This is the Big Bang: not a bang in the usual sense, not an explosion *into* anything, but the hot dense origin of space, time, and everything in them. It sounds like the wildest of speculations. Yet it is one of the best-tested theories in all of science, because the early universe left behind a relic we can still detect today — a faint hiss of microwaves coming from every direction in the sky, the literal afterglow of creation. This lesson is the story of that fire and its ashes.

## The hot early universe

The logic is simply the expanding universe in reverse. Today space is cold and sparse; the average temperature of empty space is $2.725\ \text{K}$. Wind the scale factor back and everything compresses and heats. A second after the beginning, the universe was a uniform soup at roughly ten billion kelvin — hotter than the centre of any star — too hot for atoms or even atomic nuclei to survive. Free protons, neutrons, electrons, photons, and neutrinos slammed into one another billions of times a second in a dense, glowing fog.

The key physics is that as the universe expands, it **cools**. The cosmological redshift that stretches the wavelength of light also stretches the wavelengths of all the photons filling space, draining their energy. So the history of the universe is a history of *cooling through thresholds* — each drop in temperature allowing some new kind of structure, previously shaken apart, to survive. The first few of those thresholds, crossed in the opening minutes, set the chemical stage for everything to follow.

## Nucleosynthesis: forging the first elements

In the first second, the universe was too hot for protons and neutrons to stick — any nucleus that formed was instantly blasted apart by the surrounding heat. But as expansion cooled the soup, a brief window opened. Between roughly one second and a few minutes after the beginning, at around a billion kelvin, protons and neutrons could finally bind without being torn apart. They fused — in essentially the same nuclear reactions that power stars — into the lightest nuclei. This is **Big Bang nucleosynthesis**.

By the time the universe had cooled too far to fuse anything more (a window only minutes wide), the score was set: about **75% hydrogen and 25% helium** by mass, with a trace of lithium and deuterium, and almost nothing heavier. (Everything heavier than that — the carbon, oxygen, iron, and gold — came much later, forged in stars and supernovae, as the stellar-death lesson described.) This primordial recipe is a sharp, falsifiable prediction, and it is one of the great triumphs of the theory: the observed abundance of helium and the trace of deuterium in the oldest, most pristine gas clouds match the Big Bang calculation to remarkable precision. The universe really did start cooking light elements in its first three minutes.

:::key Two clocks, two kitchens
Big Bang nucleosynthesis made the universe's hydrogen and helium in the first minutes. Stellar fusion and supernovae made nearly everything heavier, over billions of years. The fact that the cosmos is overwhelmingly hydrogen and helium — exactly the Big Bang's prediction — is a fingerprint of its hot origin.
:::

## Recombination and the release of the CMB

After nucleosynthesis, the universe spent the next several hundred thousand years as a hot, opaque plasma. Crucially, it was *opaque*. Free electrons are superb at scattering light: a photon could travel no distance at all before bouncing off an electron, so the universe was a glowing, impenetrable fog, like the inside of the Sun. Light and matter were locked together.

Then, about **380,000 years** after the beginning, the temperature fell to around $3000\ \text{K}$ — cool enough, at last, for electrons to be captured by protons and bound into stable, neutral **hydrogen atoms**. This moment is called **recombination**. With the free electrons suddenly mopped up into atoms, the fog lifted: photons, no longer constantly scattering, were free to stream across space in straight lines. For the first time the universe became transparent.

Those photons have been travelling ever since — for 13.8 billion years — and they are still arriving. We call them the **cosmic microwave background**, the CMB. They are the oldest light in the universe, a snapshot of the moment it became transparent, a wall of glow surrounding us in every direction. The fire that emitted them was at $3000\ \text{K}$, but the universe has expanded roughly a thousandfold since, stretching that light by the same factor. So what was once the visible-and-infrared glow of a $3000\ \text{K}$ surface is now redshifted all the way down to a feeble microwave hiss at

$$ T_{\text{CMB}} = 2.725\ \text{K}, $$

less than three degrees above absolute zero. It is a near-perfect blackbody spectrum — the most perfect ever measured — exactly as a cooling thermal universe must produce.

:::history Discovered by accident
In 1964 Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson, calibrating a radio antenna at Bell Labs, could not get rid of a persistent hiss coming equally from all directions. They checked everything, even scrubbed pigeon droppings from the horn. The hiss remained. A few miles away, Princeton physicists were *predicting* exactly such a relic glow. The two groups met, the pieces clicked, and an unwanted noise turned out to be the afterglow of the Big Bang. Penzias and Wilson got the Nobel Prize.
:::

## Why the CMB is the strongest evidence

The Big Bang is not believed because it's an appealing story. It's believed because the CMB is the kind of evidence that's almost impossible to fake. Consider what the theory demanded *before* the glow was found: a hot, dense early universe must have been a thermal radiator; it must have become transparent at a definite moment; and that ancient light, redshifted by the expansion, must still bathe us today as a faint, uniform, blackbody microwave glow at a few kelvin. The prediction was specific. And there it is — filling the sky, at $2.725\ \text{K}$, with a blackbody spectrum so flawless that no alternative theory has ever explained it. A static or eternal universe has no reason to produce such a thing. The CMB is, quite simply, the strongest evidence we have that the universe had a hot beginning.

## Tiny ripples, vast consequences

Look closely — as the satellites COBE, WMAP, and Planck did, with exquisite precision — and the CMB is *not* perfectly uniform. Across the sky its temperature varies by about one part in 100,000: patches a hair hotter, patches a hair colder. These minuscule **anisotropies** are the most important imperfections in the history of science. They are a snapshot of the slightly-denser and slightly-sparser regions of the infant universe — the seeds.

Gravity does the rest. A region that began very slightly denser than average pulls in a little extra matter, growing denser still, pulling in more, in a slow runaway over hundreds of millions of years. Those overdense seeds grew into the first stars, then galaxies, then the vast filaments and clusters of the cosmic web we see today. The galaxy you live in, and every structure in the night sky, grew from one of those faint warm spots in the baby picture of the cosmos. The detailed statistical pattern of the ripples even lets cosmologists read off the universe's age, its geometry (flat, to high precision), and its inventory of ordinary matter, dark matter, and dark energy — the precision-cosmology numbers that anchor the rest of this track.

## What the Big Bang does and doesn't claim

It is worth being careful about what this theory actually says — because it is widely misunderstood. The Big Bang theory describes the universe's **evolution** from an extremely hot, dense early state onward: the cooling, the nucleosynthesis, the recombination, the CMB, the growth of structure. All of that is on tremendously firm observational ground.

It does **not** claim to explain the absolute first instant — the $t = 0$ "moment of creation." It does not say what "banged," or why, or what (if anything) came before, or whether the question even makes sense. Those edges run into the unsolved problem of quantum gravity (the frontier of the Graduate track), where general relativity and quantum mechanics collide and neither alone can be trusted. And the Big Bang is emphatically *not* an explosion of matter flying outward into pre-existing empty space — it is the expansion of space itself, happening everywhere at once, with no centre and no edge. The honest summary: we have an extraordinarily well-tested account of the universe from a tiny fraction of a second onward, written in the light of the cosmic microwave background — and a genuine, humbling mystery about its very first moment that physics has not yet cracked.
