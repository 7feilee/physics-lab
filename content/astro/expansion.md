For most of human history the universe was assumed to be eternal and static — a fixed stage on which the stars wheeled but the cosmos as a whole simply *was*. Then, in the 1920s, a quiet measurement of the light from faint smudges of nebulae overturned that assumption completely. The galaxies, it turned out, are nearly all rushing away from us, and the farther one lies, the faster it flees. The universe is not a stage. It is expanding — every part of it swelling away from every other part — and if you run that expansion backwards, everything we see converges, billions of years ago, into a single searing point. This is the discovery that gave us a universe with a beginning, an age, and a history.

## Hubble's law: the farther, the faster

In 1929 Edwin Hubble, working with the largest telescope on Earth, measured two things for a few dozen galaxies: their distance, and their **redshift** — how much the lines in their spectra were stretched toward longer wavelengths (the spectral barcodes from the starlight lesson, shifted bodily to the red). Redshift signals recession. Plotting recession speed against distance, Hubble found a stunningly simple straight line:

$$ v = H_0\, d. $$

A galaxy's recession speed $v$ is just its distance $d$ times a constant. That constant, the **Hubble constant**, is measured today at

$$ H_0 \approx 70\ \frac{\text{km/s}}{\text{Mpc}}, $$

meaning a galaxy one megaparsec away (about 3.26 million light-years) recedes at roughly $70\ \text{km/s}$, one two megaparsecs away at $140\ \text{km/s}$, and so on. Double the distance, double the speed. It is one of the most consequential equations ever written down, because the only natural way to get *everything* receding with speed proportional to distance is if space itself is uniformly stretching.

:::key Hubble's law in one breath
$v = H_0 d$: every galaxy recedes, and recession speed is proportional to distance. This is not an explosion *into* space from a centre — it's space itself expanding, everywhere, all at once.
:::

## Not an explosion: the raisin bread and the balloon

The deepest misconception about cosmic expansion is to picture a bomb going off, with galaxies as shrapnel flying outward from some central point that *we* happen to sit near. That is wrong, and two homely analogies fix it.

Imagine a lump of **raisin-bread dough** rising in the oven. As the dough swells, every raisin moves away from every other raisin. Pick any raisin to stand on, and you'll see all the others receding — and a raisin twice as far will recede twice as fast, because there's twice as much expanding dough between you and it. No raisin is the centre; the expansion is happening *everywhere*. Or picture dots inked on a **balloon** as you blow it up: the rubber surface (a 2D analogy for our 3D space) stretches, every dot drifts from every other, and no dot is special. We are not at the centre of the expansion. There is no centre. Every observer in every galaxy sees exactly what we see.

## Cosmological redshift: stretching, not Doppler

Here a subtle but crucial correction. The redshift of distant galaxies is *not* really the ordinary Doppler effect — galaxies are not flying through space like a passing siren. The light is **cosmologically redshifted**: while a photon travels across the cosmos, space itself expands beneath it, stretching the photon's wavelength along with everything else. A wave that left a distant galaxy when the universe was half its present size arrives with its wavelength doubled.

This gives a beautifully direct relationship. If we write the relative size of space as the **scale factor** $a(t)$ — set to $a = 1$ today and smaller in the past — then the wavelength of any travelling light stretches in exact proportion:

$$ 1 + z = \frac{\lambda_{\text{observed}}}{\lambda_{\text{emitted}}} = \frac{a(\text{now})}{a(\text{then})}. $$

So the redshift $z$ is literally a measure of how much the universe has grown since the light set out. A galaxy at $z = 1$ emitted its light when the universe was half its current size; the most distant galaxies we see, at $z \approx 11$, shine to us from when space was about one-twelfth its present extent. Redshift is a cosmic clock and a cosmic ruler in one.

## Running the film backwards

Here is the inference that changed cosmology forever. If space is expanding and everything is flying apart today, then *yesterday* everything was closer together, and a billion years ago closer still. Rewind the cosmic film and the galaxies converge, the gas between them compresses and heats, and as the scale factor $a(t)$ shrinks toward zero, the entire observable universe is squeezed into a state of enormous density and temperature. There must have been a moment — about **13.8 billion years ago** — when the universe was unimaginably hot, dense, and small. This is the **Big Bang**: not an explosion in space, but the hot, dense beginning *of* space and time themselves.

We can even estimate *when* from the expansion rate alone. The inverse of the Hubble constant, $1/H_0$, has units of time and works out to roughly 14 billion years — the **Hubble time**, a rough age of the universe. (The precise figure, 13.8 billion years, comes from accounting for how the expansion rate has changed over cosmic history, speeding and slowing as different ingredients dominated.) That a simple slope on a graph of galaxy speeds yields the age of everything is one of the quiet miracles of physics.

:::note A finite age means a horizon
Because the universe has a finite age and light travels at finite speed, we can only see out to the distance light has had time to reach us — the **observable universe**, a sphere about 46 billion light-years in radius (larger than 13.8 billion light-years precisely *because* space stretched while the light was in transit). There may be vastly more universe beyond, forever out of view. Cosmology is the science of the part we can, in principle, see.
:::

## What the expansion does and doesn't tell us

Hubble's law and the scale factor hand us a profound and well-tested picture: a universe of finite age that began hot and dense and has been expanding and cooling ever since. The evidence — every distant galaxy redshifted, the relationship dead-straight, the inferred age consistent with the oldest stars — is overwhelming. But the expansion alone doesn't tell us *what* was happening in those first hot moments, *what* the universe is made of, or *how* it will end. For the first question we turn next to the Big Bang and the relic glow it left behind; the last two questions — the dark matter that shapes the cosmic web, and the dark energy now accelerating the expansion you just watched — carry us to the open frontier. The galaxies, by simply running away, have set the entire agenda of modern cosmology.
